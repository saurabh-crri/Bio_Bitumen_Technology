CSIR Bio-Bitumen Technology

A Sustainable Response to Bitumen Imports and Stubble Burning

This QR page presents a concise technical overview of CSIR-CRRI’s bio-bitumen technology developed using pyrolysis of rice straw (parali) for flexible pavement construction.

National Context & Need

India’s road infrastructure programme relies heavily on petroleum-based paving bitumen. Nearly 49% of national demand is met through imports, resulting in an annual foreign exchange outgo of approximately ₹25,000 crore.

Concurrently, India generates over 600 million tonnes of agricultural residue annually. Open-field burning of rice straw leads to severe air pollution, greenhouse gas emissions, soil nutrient loss, and public health concerns.

A convergent solution addressing both bitumen scarcity and stubble burning is therefore essential for resilient and sustainable road development.

Core Challenges

  • High dependence on imported petroleum bitumen
  • Exposure to global crude oil price volatility
  • Supply-chain risks for large highway programmes
  • Environmental and health impacts of stubble burning

The CSIR Solution: Bio-Bitumen

CSIR-CRRI, in collaboration with CSIR-Indian Institute of Petroleum (IIP), has developed a bio-bitumen technology using pyrolysis of rice straw. The resulting bio-oil is chemically upgraded to produce a bio-binder suitable for flexible pavement applications.

Rice Straw → Pyrolysis → Bio-Oil → Bio-Binder → Road Construction

Technology Process Overview

Pyrolysis

Controlled thermal decomposition of rice straw produces bio-char, bio-oil, and gaseous products.

Bio-Binder Production

Bio-oil undergoes chemical processing to obtain a binder compatible with VG-grade bitumen.

Replacement Potential

Laboratory and field studies demonstrate up to 30% replacement of conventional bitumen.

Performance Evaluation (Laboratory)

Bio-bitumen mixes were evaluated at CSIR-CRRI against key performance parameters relevant to National Highway applications.

Indirect Tensile Strength

Comparable tensile strength behaviour.

Resilient Modulus

Stiffness within IRC-acceptable limits.

Rutting Resistance

Comparable resistance to permanent deformation.

Moisture Resistance

~99% coating retention after boiling test.

Field Demonstration

A bio-bitumen trial section was successfully constructed on 26 October 2024 on NH-6 (Jorabat–Shillong Expressway), marking one of the world’s first National Highway applications of bio-bitumen.

Impact & National Relevance

  • Potential annual savings of ₹4,000–4,500 crore
  • ~70% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions
  • Value addition to agricultural residue
  • Alignment with Waste-to-Wealth Mission
